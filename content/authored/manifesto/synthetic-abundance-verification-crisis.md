# Synthetic Abundance Creates A Verification Crisis

The verification crisis starts when convincing information becomes cheaper to produce than to evaluate. A fake source can look polished. A bot narrative can look popular. A synthetic paper, video, or project page can borrow the surface area of credibility without paying the old cost of production.

Neelo's source argues that this breaks the old filter. Before AI, making complex false information required time, craft, and coordination. After AI, the marginal cost can approach zero. The result is asymmetric cost: producing noise is cheap, but proving what deserves trust remains expensive.

Markets add cost back into belief. They do not create perfect truth. They make belief more expensive, observable, and contestable. Someone who wants to defend a claim can buy, LP, quote, or build around it. Someone who rejects a claim can short, refuse to quote, hedge, or pressure the market over time.

That negative side is essential. Long-only markets can amplify excitement even when skepticism is widespread. Two-sided markets force the claim to face both enthusiasm and rejection. In the Information/Trade thesis, Vibe's short side is the mechanism that turns synthetic abundance from pure attention warfare into a more adversarial market.

## Reader Implication

Market verification should be presented as evidence, not certainty. Vibe does not magically identify truth. It makes support, skepticism, liquidity, durability, and refusal visible enough for readers, solvers, and venues to inspect.
