# Perceived Versus Actual Listing Interest

The listing problem begins with a distinction that is easy to miss: actual interest only exists after a market can be used.

Actual interest is real buying, selling, retention, volatility, short demand, hedging demand, and repeat participation. A venue cannot observe that directly before it lists an asset. It can only observe perceived interest: community size, engagement, deposits, relationships, narrative strength, brand credibility, expected volume, prior venues, and modeled safety.

Those proxies are not useless. They are often the only inputs a curator has. But once market access depends on them, projects learn to optimize for the proxy instead of the market. The venue is trying to forecast demand. The project is trying to appear forecastable.

Neelo's game-theory point is that this is structural. Any venue with listing cost, curation responsibility, or reputational downside has to decide before actual interest is revealed. That turns listing from a market-discovery process into a prediction process.

## Reader Implication

Readers should understand why Vibe's thesis is not only "list more assets." The stronger claim is that early derivative markets create the missing observation layer. They let actual interest become visible before later venues, solvers, and liquidity providers have to rely entirely on perceived interest.
