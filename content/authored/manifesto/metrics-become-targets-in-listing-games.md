# Metrics Become Targets In Listing Games

When market access depends on perceived-interest proxies, those proxies become targets.

The source lists the usual substitutes for actual demand: community size, engagement, deposits, fees, expected volume, narrative momentum, institutional credibility, and modeled safety. A venue uses those signals because actual interest cannot be observed before a market exists. Projects then optimize for the signals because those signals control access.

This is not a minor analytics problem. It changes project behavior. Teams learn to look safer, larger, more active, more liquid, and more inevitable before the market has had a chance to test them. Marketing, social metrics, deposits, partnerships, valuation optics, and venue relationships become part of the listing game.

## The Goodhart Problem

The compendium should explain this as a Goodhart-like loop without overstating it. Metrics are useful until they become the gate. Once they become the gate, the system begins rewarding the appearance of demand alongside, and sometimes ahead of, demand itself.

That is why derivative access matters. A live two-sided market produces harder evidence than a pre-listing proxy: who trades, who hedges, who shorts, how long positions persist, how liquidations behave, and whether solver support is sustainable. Those signals are not perfect, but they are closer to actual interest because they come from capital at risk.

The answer to proxy gaming is not no metrics. It is better metrics produced by real market behavior.
