# The Verification Breaking Point

Neelo's Information and Trade introduction names a hard problem: when synthetic information becomes cheap, familiar verification systems lose force.

The source uses deliberately strong language. Experts can be imitated. Brands can be manufactured. Polls, articles, and public claims can be produced at scale. The compendium should preserve the pressure of that argument while translating it into a precise mechanism: information needs costly signals when surface credibility becomes cheap.

That is where market structure enters the thesis. A market cap is not objective truth. It is not moral authority. It is a costly public signal that many actors have attached capital to a claim, token, project, or narrative. In a low-cost information environment, that cost matters because it is harder to fake than a post, a logo, or a polished story.

## From Proof Of Authority To Proof Of Value

The source frames the shift as Proof of Authority to Proof of Value. Authority-based verification asks who said something. Value-based verification asks what survives when capital can support, reject, hedge, short, and reprice it.

For Vibe, that distinction matters because tokenized information needs more than a buy button. If a claim can only attract believers, market cap can overstate reality. If it can also be shorted, hedged, and stress-tested, it becomes more expensive to maintain a false signal.

## Publication Boundary

Do not publish "market cap equals truth" as a literal claim. The source supports a costly-signal thesis: markets can make claims harder to fake, but they can still be manipulated, reflexive, illiquid, or wrong. Public prose should keep the difference between market evidence and objective truth explicit.
