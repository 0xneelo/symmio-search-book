# Market Cap Is A Costly Signal, Not Perfect Truth

The safe version of the market-cap thesis is not "price proves truth." It is that market cap can be a costly signal in a world where unsupported content is cheap.

Market cap reveals commitment. Liquidity reveals whether other participants are willing to trade around that commitment. Durability reveals whether the claim survives more than the first wave of attention. Short interest, funding, solver appetite, and liquidation behavior reveal whether skepticism has enough force to matter.

Those signals are useful precisely because they cost something. They are harder to fake than a polished post, a synthetic video, or a narrative campaign. But they are still signals. Crowds can be wrong. Rich participants can distort markets. Coordinated narratives can persist longer than they deserve.

The signal improves when markets have two sides. A long-only market can confuse enthusiasm with validation. A market with shorting, solver refusal, hedging, and leverage is harder to flatter because capital can attack the claim instead of only opting out.

## Reader Implication

If someone asks whether market cap proves truth, the answer is no. Market cap is evidence of capitalized belief. Vibe's thesis is stronger when the docs treat price as inspectable evidence inside an adversarial market, not as an infallible oracle.
