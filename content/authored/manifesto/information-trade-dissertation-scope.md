# Information And Trade Dissertation Scope

The Information and Trade dissertation is a map of why Vibe belongs in the compendium's manifesto, not only in its product reference.

Neelo's scope section lists six connected questions. First, what happens when synthetic abundance breaks old verification systems? Second, why do prediction markets fail to cover the long tail of claims, assets, and narratives? Third, why does a real market need a NO button: short-side expression available early enough to matter? Fourth, what architecture can support that without pretending every token has deep order-book liquidity on day one?

The final two questions connect market structure to strategy and product language. Listing control becomes a lifecycle problem: who gives assets their first serious derivative market, and who turns that early evidence into downstream trust? The Thumbs Down becomes the public metaphor for financialized rejection in the tokenized economy.

## Why Scope Matters

This scope keeps readers from misunderstanding the thesis. The dissertation is not only saying "prediction markets are limited" or "shorting is useful." It is tying verification, token issuance, leverage, intents, solvers, listing games, and negative conviction into one architecture question.

That is why this source belongs in the answer engine. A reader who asks for the whole Information/Trade arc needs a page that points across the crisis, the NO button, intent-based execution, and the token lifecycle rather than landing on one isolated concept.

## Publication Boundary

Do not imply that every scoped item is currently implemented as a production product surface. Treat the scope as the source's intellectual agenda. Product availability, listing policy, leverage, solver behavior, risk controls, and UI metaphors need current implementation review before publication.
