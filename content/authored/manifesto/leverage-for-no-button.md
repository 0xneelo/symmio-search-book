# Why The NO Button Needs Leverage

The Missing NO Button source argues that negative conviction needs enough force to matter.

In a long-only market, a false or overhyped claim can attract many small believers while informed skeptics can only abstain or complain outside the market. The source's example is intentionally simple: a crowd with small contributions can push a lie higher, while a better-informed minority may not have enough spot capital to counter it.

Leverage changes that information geometry. It lets high-conviction capital express a larger view than its cash balance alone would allow. In the NO-button thesis, that matters because truth, or at least better information, may sit with a minority. If the minority cannot act at sufficient scale, market price can remain a popularity signal instead of a stress-tested signal.

## The Necessary Caveat

Leverage does not make the minority right. It amplifies conviction, and conviction can be wrong, manipulative, or undercollateralized. That is why this thesis only works inside risk controls: margin, liquidation, solver quote discipline, borrow constraints, open-interest caps, and refusal paths.

The publication-safe answer is therefore precise: leverage is necessary for negative information to compete with hype, but leverage is only useful if the system can contain wrong or adversarial leveraged views.

## Publication Boundary

Do not publish live leverage levels, eligibility, liquidation thresholds, borrow mechanics, market caps, or shorting availability without current product and risk review. This page explains the source's argument for why short-side conviction needs amplification.
